The Attorney Review Period in New York Real Estate — What Buyers and Sellers Need to Know

New York works differently than New Jersey. There is no 3-day review window — but attorneys still control everything. Here is exactly how it works.

Key takeaway: New York has no formal attorney review period after signing. Instead, the contract is negotiated between attorneys before anyone signs. You cannot be legally bound to a purchase until the contract is fully executed and delivered — which protects both sides, just in a different way than New Jersey's post-signing review.

How New York Is Different from New Jersey

If you have bought or sold property in New Jersey, you are familiar with the three-day attorney review period — a window after the contract is signed during which either party's attorney can cancel or modify the agreement. It is a built-in safety net that exists by custom and practice in NJ transactions.

New York works completely differently. In NY, the contract is not signed until both attorneys have reviewed and agreed to the terms. There is no post-signing review window because the review happens upfront. The buyer and seller do not sign until the attorneys have finished negotiating — which means by the time signatures hit the paper, both sides have already approved every term.

This is the most common source of confusion for buyers who have experience purchasing property in other states. In New York, the sequence is: negotiate first, then sign. Not the other way around.

The Step-by-Step Contract Process in New York

In New York residential real estate, the purchase contract process flows through attorneys on both sides. Here is the typical sequence from accepted offer to fully executed contract:

1

Offer Accepted

Buyer and seller agree verbally on price, closing date, and basic terms. A deal sheet or binder is often prepared by the real estate agent. At this stage, nothing is legally binding — either side can still walk away.

2

Seller's Attorney Drafts the Contract

The seller's attorney prepares the initial contract of sale, typically based on a standard form with customized riders addressing the specific terms of this deal — mortgage contingency, inspection rights, inclusions and exclusions, closing timeline.

3

Purchaser's Attorney Reviews and Negotiates

The buyer's attorney receives the draft contract and reviews it line by line. They will typically mark up the contract with changes, add protective riders, and negotiate terms with the seller's attorney. This is where the real work happens — and where the buyer's interests are protected.

4

Back-and-Forth Negotiation

The attorneys go back and forth — sometimes for days, sometimes for weeks — until both sides agree on the final terms. Every change is tracked. Every rider is reviewed. This process traditionally happens by email, phone, and sometimes fax — a fragmented workflow that platforms like ArpyEscrow's deal room are designed to replace.

5

Buyer Signs the Contract

Once the purchaser's attorney is satisfied with the terms, the buyer signs the contract and submits their deposit — typically 10% of the purchase price — which is held in the seller's attorney's escrow account.

6

Seller Countersigns — Contract Is Fully Executed

The seller countersigns. The contract is now fully executed and legally binding. The deal moves forward to mortgage commitment, appraisal, inspections, and eventually closing. The 45-day mortgage commitment clock starts now.

The Delivery Requirement — Why Timing Matters

In New York, there is a legal doctrine called the delivery requirement. Even if both parties have signed, the contract is not technically binding until the signed copy is delivered back to the purchaser or the purchaser's attorney. This is why you will hear attorneys say a deal is not "in contract" until they confirm receipt of the countersigned copy.

This matters for several reasons. First, the 45-day mortgage commitment deadline does not start until delivery. Second, either party can theoretically back out between signing and delivery — though this is rare and considered bad faith. Third, disputes about when delivery occurred can affect the enforceability of the entire contract.

What the Purchaser's Attorney Reviews

Since there is no time limit on the pre-signing review in New York, buyers have the opportunity to be thorough. A good purchaser's attorney will scrutinize:

Title issues — liens, encumbrances, easements, or deed restrictions that could affect your use of the property or ability to get a mortgage. Mortgage contingency language — specifically, the 45-day mortgage commitment deadline and what happens if you cannot get financing approved in time. Inspection contingency — whether you have the right to inspect and negotiate repairs, or if the property is sold strictly as-is. Inclusions and exclusions — what exactly conveys with the property: appliances, light fixtures, window treatments, storage units. STAR exemption and tax status — current property taxes and any pending tax challenges. HOA or co-op rules — if buying in a condo or co-op, board approval requirements, sublet restrictions, and maintenance fees.

The Seller's Perspective

From the seller's side, the period between an accepted offer and a fully executed contract is one of the most nerve-wracking parts of the transaction. The buyer has not formally committed yet — and until that contract is signed and delivered, the deal is not real.

Sellers often worry about buyers using the review period to shop for a better deal, negotiate endlessly to extract concessions, or simply ghost after weeks of negotiation. A good seller's attorney will push for a reasonable timeline to complete the review and will advise the seller on when to hold firm and when to compromise.

This is another area where a deal room adds value. When every document exchange is tracked and timestamped, both sides have visibility into the pace of negotiation — and neither side can credibly claim they are waiting on the other when the record shows otherwise.

Do You Need an Attorney in New York?

While New York does not legally require an attorney for a real estate transaction, it is the strong custom and standard practice for both buyers and sellers to be represented by their own attorney. The contract negotiation process in NY is designed to be handled by attorneys, and proceeding without one is considered risky and unusual.

Attorney fees in New York residential transactions are typically a flat fee ranging from $1,500 to $3,500, depending on the complexity of the deal and the attorney's experience level. Considering the dollar amounts at stake in a property purchase — and the legal protections an attorney provides — it is one of the best investments in the entire transaction.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does New York have an attorney review period like New Jersey?

No. New York does not have a formal 3-day attorney review period like New Jersey. In NY, the buyer's attorney reviews and negotiates the contract before any signatures are exchanged. The contract is not binding until both sides sign, so the review happens upfront rather than after signing.

When does a real estate contract become binding in New York?

A New York real estate contract becomes legally binding only after both the buyer and seller have signed the same copy of the contract and the fully executed contract has been delivered to both parties.

How long does attorney review take in New York?

New York attorney review typically takes 1 to 4 weeks, depending on how many changes need to be negotiated and how quickly both sides respond. A deal with minimal negotiation can close in a few days. Complex deals or properties with title issues can take 4 to 6 weeks or longer.

Can a buyer walk away before the contract is signed?

Yes. Until both parties have signed the same contract and it has been delivered, either side can walk away with no legal obligation and no financial penalty. No deposit is at risk until the contract is fully executed.

Do I need a real estate attorney in New York?

While not legally required, it is the strong custom and standard practice. The contract process in NY is designed to be handled by attorneys, and proceeding without one is considered risky. Fees typically range from $1,500 to $3,500.

What is a rider in a New York real estate contract?

A rider is an addendum to the standard form contract that addresses specific terms for your deal — like the mortgage contingency, inspection rights, or what happens if there are title defects. Riders are where most of the substantive negotiation happens.

How much does a real estate attorney cost in New York?

Attorney fees in New York residential transactions are typically a flat fee ranging from $1,500 to $3,500, depending on the complexity of the deal and the attorney's experience level.

Mortgage Commitment

The 45-Day Mortgage Commitment Deadline in New York — What Buyers and Attorneys Need to Know

This is the most dangerous deadline in a New York real estate transaction. Miss it, and you could lose your entire deposit.

Key takeaway: In most New York residential contracts, the buyer has 45 days from the date of full contract execution to obtain a mortgage commitment letter from their lender. If the buyer fails to secure financing within this window and does not request an extension, they may lose the right to cancel under the mortgage contingency — and forfeit a deposit that is typically 10% of the purchase price.

What Is the 45-Day Mortgage Commitment?

The 45-day mortgage commitment is a deadline written into the standard residential real estate contract used in New York transactions. It establishes a window — typically 45 days from the date the contract is fully executed and delivered — within which the buyer must obtain a mortgage commitment letter from their lender.

A mortgage commitment letter is not the same as a pre-approval. A pre-approval is a preliminary indication that a lender is willing to lend based on basic financial information. A commitment letter is a formal, binding statement from the lender that the buyer has been approved for a specific loan amount on a specific property, subject to final conditions like the appraisal and title clearance.

The 45-day window exists to protect both sides. The buyer gets time to finalize their financing. The seller gets assurance that the buyer is serious and that the deal will either move forward or be formally terminated within a defined period — rather than dragging on indefinitely while the buyer shops for a better rate or reconsiders the purchase.

When Does the Clock Start?

This is one of the most commonly misunderstood aspects of the timeline. The 45-day clock does not start when the offer is accepted. It does not start when the buyer signs the contract. It starts when the contract is fully executed — meaning both the buyer and the seller have signed the same copy — and delivered to both parties.

In New York, there is a legal doctrine called the delivery requirement. Even if both parties have signed, the contract is not technically binding until the signed copy is delivered back to the purchaser or the purchaser's attorney. This is why you will hear attorneys say a deal is not "in contract" until they confirm receipt of the countersigned copy.

The practical implication: if there is a gap of several days between the buyer signing and the seller countersigning and delivering, those days do not count against the 45-day window. The clock starts on the date of delivery, not the date of the buyer's signature.

What Happens If the Buyer Misses the Deadline?

If the buyer does not obtain a mortgage commitment within the 45-day window — and has not requested and received an extension — the consequences can be severe:

The mortgage contingency may expire. Most New York contracts include a mortgage contingency clause that allows the buyer to cancel the contract and receive their deposit back if they cannot obtain financing. Once the 45-day window passes without a commitment letter or an extension, this protection may evaporate.

The deposit is at risk. The standard contract deposit in New York is typically 10% of the purchase price. On a $500,000 home, that is $50,000. On a $1,000,000 home, that is $100,000. If the mortgage contingency has expired and the buyer cannot close, the seller may have the right to keep the entire deposit as liquidated damages.

The seller may cancel the contract. Some contracts give the seller the right to terminate the deal if the buyer has not produced a commitment letter within the specified period. Even if the contract does not grant automatic termination, a missed deadline gives the seller significant leverage in any renegotiation.

Can the Deadline Be Extended?

Yes — and extensions are common. The process is straightforward: the buyer's attorney sends a written request to the seller's attorney asking for additional time, typically 15 to 30 days. The seller is not obligated to agree, but in practice most sellers will grant a reasonable extension rather than risk losing the deal entirely.

The critical point is that the extension request must happen before the original deadline expires. If the buyer waits until day 46 to ask for more time, the mortgage contingency may have already lapsed — and the seller is under no obligation to revive it.

This is why tracking the exact date of contract execution and delivery is so important. A one-day miscalculation can be the difference between a protected buyer and a buyer who has just lost their deposit.

Typical Mortgage Commitment Timeline

1

Day 0 — Contract Fully Executed and Delivered

Both sides have signed. The countersigned contract is delivered. The 45-day clock starts now.

2

Days 1–7 — Loan Application Submitted

The buyer completes their formal mortgage application and submits all required documentation to the lender. Speed matters here — every day of delay compresses the remaining window.

3

Days 7–21 — Underwriting and Appraisal

The lender begins underwriting. An appraisal is ordered to confirm the property value supports the loan amount. This is where most delays happen — understaffed lenders, appraisal scheduling, and document follow-ups can consume weeks.

4

Days 21–35 — Conditional Approval

The lender issues a conditional approval, listing outstanding items that must be resolved before the commitment letter is issued. The buyer's attorney should be monitoring this closely.

5

Days 35–45 — Commitment Letter Issued

If all conditions are satisfied, the lender issues the mortgage commitment letter. The buyer's attorney notifies the seller's attorney, and the deal moves forward toward closing.

!

Day 45 — Deadline

If no commitment letter has been obtained and no extension has been requested, the mortgage contingency may expire. The buyer's deposit is now at risk.

Protecting Yourself as a Buyer

Apply for your mortgage immediately after the contract is signed. Do not wait a week to get organized. Every day you delay compresses the lender's window to underwrite, appraise, and issue the commitment letter.

Stay responsive to your lender. The single most common cause of mortgage delays is the buyer failing to provide documents the lender has requested — tax returns, bank statements, employment verification, explanations of large deposits. Respond within 24 hours of any lender request.

Know your deadline. Ask your attorney to confirm the exact date the 45-day clock started and when it expires. Write it down. Put it on your calendar. Ask your attorney to confirm it in writing.

Request an extension early. If your lender signals that the commitment letter might not arrive in time, tell your attorney immediately. The earlier the extension request goes out, the more likely the seller's side will agree to it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the 45-day mortgage commitment in New York?

It is a standard contractual deadline requiring the buyer to obtain a mortgage commitment letter from their lender within 45 days of the contract being fully executed. If the buyer cannot secure financing within this window, the mortgage contingency clause typically allows them to cancel and receive their deposit back.

What happens if the buyer misses the deadline?

The buyer may lose their right to cancel under the mortgage contingency and could forfeit their contract deposit — typically 10% of the purchase price — if they are ultimately unable to close.

Can the 45-day deadline be extended?

Yes. Extensions are common and are negotiated between the attorneys. The buyer's attorney requests an extension in writing before the original deadline expires. Most sellers will agree to a reasonable extension rather than risk losing the deal.

When does the 45-day clock start?

The clock starts when the contract is fully executed — meaning both parties have signed and the signed contract has been delivered. It does not start when the offer is accepted or when the buyer alone signs.

What is a mortgage commitment letter?

A formal document from a lender stating that the buyer has been approved for a specific loan amount, subject to certain conditions. It is stronger than a pre-approval and indicates the lender's intent to fund the loan.
